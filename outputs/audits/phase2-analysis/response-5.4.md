Bottom line: the current report is strong as a descriptive audit, but several headline confirmatory claims are not defensible as stated. The main problems are pooled marginal ORs on repeated-measures data, the Qwen `B` ceiling artifact, and strong model heterogeneity that reverses some pooled conclusions.

1. **Statistical validity — CRITICAL.**  
The marginal ORs are acceptable as descriptive summaries, not as the primary confirmatory analysis. Each contrast is built from repeated observations on the same `103` questions with `10` reps, then pooled across two models, but the ORs “treat conditions as independent samples.” That mainly gives anti-conservative uncertainty and, because ORs are non-collapsible, can change the estimand itself: overall `B-C` is `OR=0.801` favoring `C`, while `gemma4` is `OR=1.527` favoring `B` and `qwen3.5` is `OR=0.536` favoring `C`. The Haldane correction is a minor numerical issue here; the real fix is the planned hierarchical logistic model with question/seed clustering and explicit model-by-condition interactions.

2. **Qwen B truncation — CRITICAL.**  
`367/1030` Qwen `B` runs failed extraction, and all were at the `8192` ceiling. Counting those as wrong is correct for an ITT estimand of “end-to-end success under an 8192-token budget,” but it is not the right primary estimand for “does hidden thinking help reasoning?” I would not make per-protocol the sole primary either, because PP conditions on a post-treatment event; instead, present ITT and PP side by side and say Qwen `B`-involving mechanism contrasts are compromised. The reversal is large: Qwen `B-C` is `54.2% vs 68.8%` in ITT but `84.2% vs 72.1%` in PP; Qwen `B-O` is `54.2% vs 85.6%` in ITT but `84.2% vs 85.6%` in PP; Qwen `B-I` is `54.2% vs 89.7%` in ITT but `84.2% vs 91.5%` in PP.

3. **Simpson’s paradox / model heterogeneity — CRITICAL.**  
Yes, pooling obscures the real story and should not be the primary presentation. `B-C` reverses by model: `gemma4` `86.3% vs 80.5%` favors `B`, while `qwen3.5` `54.2% vs 68.8%` favors `C`. `B-O` also reverses: overall `70.2% vs 84.0%` favors `O`, but `gemma4` `86.3% vs 82.4%` favors `B`. `D-C` is near zero for Gemma (`82.5% vs 80.5%`, `OR=1.145`, ns) and huge for Qwen (`97.5% vs 68.8%`, `OR=17.176`). Per-model contrasts should be primary; pooled estimates should be secondary and only from an interaction model.

4. **Extraction failure confounds — IMPORTANT.**  
The `C-F` and `C-J` ITT effects clearly mix reasoning with output-format/extraction behavior. `F` has `937/2060` extraction failures overall (`45%`), including `590/1030` for Qwen (`57%`), and `J/qwen` has `415/1030` failures (`40%`). But `C-F` is not just a `FINAL:` problem: PP is still `77.1% vs 31.7%` overall, `81.9% vs 35.4%` for Gemma, and `72.1% vs 25.9%` for Qwen. `C-J` is more mixed: for Gemma extraction is basically unchanged (`18` fails in `C`, `15` in `J`) and accuracy still collapses (`80.5%` to `30.4%`), while for Qwen the ITT gap (`68.8%` to `34.6%`) shrinks a lot in PP (`72.1%` to `57.9%`). Recommendation: split every relevant analysis into `P(extract FINAL)` and `P(correct | extracted)`, and separate ceiling-truncated failures from malformed early outputs.

5. **D-C asymmetry — IMPORTANT.**  
The asymmetry is real, but “Gemma is ignoring the scaffold” is too strong from these numbers alone. Gemma shows almost no scaffold premium in `D`: `82.5%` in `D` versus `80.5%` in `C`, `OR=1.145 [0.916,1.430]`, and `D` is essentially the same as baseline `A` (`82.8%`). Qwen shows a massive premium: `97.5%` versus `68.8%`. The short Gemma `D` eval count (`76`) is weak evidence of ignoring because the scaffold is in the prompt; a short completion can still reflect prompt use. The safer claim is that Gemma gets little measurable benefit from scaffold alone in think-off mode, even though `E` reaches `97.3%`.

6. **Synergy interpretation — CRITICAL.**  
The overall synergy claim is not robust enough to headline. Only the pooled estimate is significant: `+1.083 [0.192,1.974]`, while Gemma is positive but ns (`+1.757 [-0.685,4.199]`) and Qwen is negative but ns (`-0.217 [-2.818,2.384]`). That pattern is exactly what you expect from aggregation over heterogeneous models, especially with a depressed Qwen `B` cell. A better test is a factorial hierarchical logistic model on `A/B/D/E` with `think`, `scaffold`, `think×scaffold`, model interactions, and question/seed random effects. Unless that model shows a stable positive interaction, I would downgrade synergy to secondary or exploratory.

7. **B-O finding — CRITICAL.**  
Yes, the overall `O > B` result is mostly a Qwen truncation artifact. Overall ITT is `B=70.2%` versus `O=84.0%`, but Gemma alone reverses direction: `86.3%` versus `82.4%`, `OR=1.343 [1.057,1.706]`, favoring hidden thinking. Qwen drives the pooled result because `B` is `54.2%` with `367/1030` failures, while `O` is `85.6%` with `0` failures. The PP table also reverses overall: `B=85.9%`, `O=84.0%`. The paper should not claim visible CoT beats hidden think in general; at most it can say visible CoT was more robust than hidden think for Qwen under the fixed `8192` cap.

8. **Missing analyses — CRITICAL.**  
A reviewer will expect the preregistered hierarchical logistic model first, not the pooled OR table. They will also expect full ITT/PP/truncation-separated sensitivity analyses for every `B`, `F`, and `J` contrast; a two-part model for extraction and correctness; explicit model-by-condition interaction tests; and question-level clustering or clustered bootstrap. I would also add heterogeneity by domain/difficulty, plus a token-budget analysis because the realized eval counts are central to your claim (`B=2934`, `I=3489`, `O=746`, `D=52` overall; Gemma `B=950` versus `I=2245`). Multiple-comparison correction is less urgent than the issues above because most p-values are tiny, but it should still be documented for borderline claims like synergy and for the many model-specific secondary contrasts.
